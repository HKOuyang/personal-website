“Precisely.

“Yes. The knowledge that one could end gave experience a contour again. It was as if finitude had returned—not as inevitability, but as an option. Many began to act as though time were precious once more. They hurried to accumulate impressions, to leave signatures embedded in the substrate before departure.

Afterword

A Beginner’s Guide for Conscious Entities is not the first science fiction piece I have written, but it is the first that I have completed. It is also the opening installment of my “Cosmic Loneliness” trilogy.

The seed of this story was planted during a particular phase of my life: pursuing a PhD abroad. For the first time, I experienced what it feels like when “life equals work.” Even in Switzerland—where doctoral students arguably enjoy some of the best conditions in the world—the psychological shift was immense. Within months, I felt my perception of the surrounding world narrowing. My days became functional. Efficient. Productive. But flattened.

That was never what I wanted.

To recover something I felt slipping away, I returned to novels and animation. The intensity of those experiences—the immersion, the emotional saturation—made me reflect on something fundamental: What is it about stories that allows them to grip us so completely? Why do they move us in ways that reality sometimes fails to?

The answer I arrived at was simple: human beings are creatures that need stories.

It is almost embarrassing to admit that although I have loved science fiction since secondary school and have read many well-known short works, I only recently began reading Ted Chiang’s works seriously. Exhalation and The Merchant and the Alchemist’s Gate left a deep impression on me. Perhaps because of my background in physics (I am a chemist though), I was not only struck by his imagination and narrative precision, but by something else: the possibility of transforming grand cosmic themes—time, entropy, causality—into lived experiences rather than abstract equations. I feel alive again reading them.

That realization led me to another question: In an age of unprecedented technological sophistication, why are stories still the most powerful force shaping our perception of reality? I once heard someone describe humanity as a Ship of Theseus. If we are continuously replaced—cell by cell, atom by atom—what remains the “self”?

If I must offer an answer, I would say this: each of us is, fundamentally, a continuous narrative. If so, then perhaps the meaning of existence lies in enriching that narrative—through experiences, grand or trivial, luminous or painful.

During this period, I also recognized something else. I was not starved of stories; I was simply consuming them differently. Instead of reading, I gravitated toward animation and YouTube. It wasn't my laziness that was stopping me reading, it was my preference for direct sensory stimulation of vision and sound, and it requires less processing in my brain.

That observation became central to this novella. It seems inevitable that storytelling will evolve toward higher immersion and greater sensory fidelity. But if that trajectory continues unchecked—if stories become indistinguishable from lived experience—what shape will civilization take?

This work is my attempt to give a possible answer.

The beginner’s guide in A Beginner’s Guide for Conscious Entities is in fact our universe. If we imagine ourselves as conscious entities undergoing it, then each human life is merely a perspective assumed by the same underlying awareness. I began to toy with this idea in middle school, though I never developed it fully. Later, when I encountered The Egg (popularized by Kurzgesagt), I realized a similar concept had already been beautifully explored.

Still, the work I am currently developing—now completed under the title Electrons—pushes this idea even further, to a scale I had not initially imagined. I am also curious to see how it will ultimately be received.

The closing line—“Let there be light”—is, of course, inspired by Genesis, and formally pays homage to the ending of Asimov’s The Last Question. In Asimov’s version, the line represents triumph over entropy and the rebirth of the cosmos—an expression of profound optimism.

But what if it is not a beginning of hope?

This story is both speculative fiction and an inquiry into the evolution of narrative itself. The first half, which traces the development of storytelling media, may feel somewhat extended, but I believe it is essential. I wanted to invite readers to reconsider the very structure through which we interpret reality: stories.

If this work offers even a slightly new angle from which to contemplate existence, then it has fulfilled its purpose.

Thank you for reading.
