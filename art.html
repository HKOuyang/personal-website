Combinatorialism

Combinatorialism is a conceptual approach to art and literature that treats combination as the medium of creation, permuting basic elements such as words, lines, or forms to explore all possible works. Originating in Ramon Llull's Ars Magna and Leibniz's De Arte Combinatoria, it reappears in Oulipo, serial art, and Borges's Library of Babel, emphasizing structure and constraint as sources of meaning.

Selected fragments from the archive

There is a hypothetical database of every kind of art form that existed and can ever exist. These entries are not illustrations of a style outside the archive. They are the archive, surfaced one name at a time.

Entry 001

Thryven

Thryven fractures measured cadence into suspended pauses, transforming directional motion into a chain of unresolved hesitations.

Entry 014

Velnora

Velnora arranges ceremonial surfaces around a missing event, allowing ornament to behave as if memory were a navigational instrument.

Entry 023

Caelith

Caelith lowers radiant structure into almost-architectural weather, where every contour appears complete yet remains politely unfinished.

Entry 041

Solivane

Solivane treats illumination as a grammatical delay, letting brightness arrive one clause too late for certainty.

Entry 067

Mernaxis

Mernaxis converts stable proportion into persuasive drift, so that each composition appears to revise the law that generated it.

Entry 102

Orithane

Orithane repeats symbolic edges until they resemble evidence from a discipline that has not yet been named.
